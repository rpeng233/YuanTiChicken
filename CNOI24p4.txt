Use symmetry and assume \(n \le m\).

All valid fractions below \(1\) have a unique finite continued-fraction form

\[
\frac1{2k_1+\frac1{2k_2+\cdots+\frac1{2k_t}}}
\]

where every \(k_i\) is a positive integer. Fractions above \(1\) are exactly their reciprocals. Thus it is enough to count these continued fractions and also count when their reciprocals fit the bounds.

To avoid enumerating every continued fraction separately, choose the first occurrence of the maximum \(k_i=x\). Then all terms before it are \(<x\), all terms after it are \(\le x\). For a fixed surrounding sequence, the resulting fraction is a linear fractional expression

\[
\frac{ax+b}{cx+d}.
\]

DFS over the surrounding sequence, maintaining \((a,b,c,d)\) and the lower bound \(v\) of \(x\). For each state, all valid \(x\ge v\) form an interval, so add them in \(O(1)\):

\[
ax+b\le n,\quad cx+d\le m
\]

counts the fraction itself, and

\[
cx+d\le n
\]

counts its reciprocal, because \(n\le m\).

When extending the DFS by adding a term \(u\), update the linear transform by

\[
(a,b,c,d)\leftarrow(c,d,2cu+a,2du+b),
\]

and update the lower bound to \(\max(v,u+1)\) for terms before the chosen maximum. A separate DFS builds the suffix after the chosen maximum, where the bound is \(\max(v,u)\).

Prune immediately when even the smallest possible \(x\) already violates the numerator or denominator bound. The number of reachable states is small enough for the constraints; use 64-bit integers for the answer and recursion-stack-only DFS memory.
